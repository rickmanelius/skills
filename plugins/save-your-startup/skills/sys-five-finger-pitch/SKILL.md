# Five-Finger Pitch

**From *Save Your Startup* by Rick Manelius (Chapter 5)**
**Framework by Chris Salter, presented in the book**

> "You should practice enough times that you could drive your car and mentally step through each finger."

## Why This Matters

Most founders either ramble for twenty minutes or freeze up in sixty seconds. The Five-Finger Pitch gives you a physical mnemonic -- one finger, one minute, five minutes total -- that covers every question an investor, partner, or customer actually cares about. No slides. No deck. Just you, your hand, and a clear story.

Five fingers. Five minutes. Five questions that matter.

## How This Works

We will walk through each finger together. For each one, I will ask you probing questions to strengthen that minute of your pitch. By the end, you will have a polished script with talking points for each finger.

Hold up your hand. We are going finger by finger.

### Finger 1 -- THUMB: Company and Product

*One minute. What is this thing?*

I will ask you:

- **What is the company name?**
- **What is the main product or service?**
- **In one sentence, what do you do?** (No jargon. If your grandmother would not understand it, simplify.)
- **What problem does this solve, and for whom?**
- **What is the single most important thing someone should remember about what you do?**

The thumb sets the foundation. If this minute is muddy, the rest falls apart.

### Finger 2 -- INDEX: Money and ROI

*One minute. How does this make money?*

I will ask you:

- **What is your business model?** (How do you charge? Who pays?)
- **What does your unit economics look like?** (Even rough numbers help.)
- **What is the ROI story for investors?** (What does the upside look like?)
- **What is your current revenue or traction?** (Zero is an honest answer.)
- **What are you raising, and what will you do with the money?**

The index finger points to the money. Investors need to see a path to returns.

### Finger 3 -- MIDDLE: Team and Feasibility

*One minute. Can it actually be done?*

I will ask you:

- **Who is the leader? Why are they the right person for this?**
- **Who else is on the team? What makes this group uniquely qualified?**
- **What have you already built or shipped?**
- **What is the hardest technical or operational challenge, and how are you handling it?**
- **What is your unfair advantage in execution?**

The middle finger is the reality check. Ideas are cheap. The question is whether this team can deliver.

### Finger 4 -- RING: Sales and Scale

*One minute. Can it be sold? Can it scale?*

I will ask you:

- **How do you acquire customers today?**
- **What does your sales process look like?**
- **What is your current pipeline or customer base?**
- **How does this scale?** (What changes when you go from 10 to 1,000 to 100,000 customers?)
- **What is the market size, and how much of it can you realistically capture?**

The ring finger is about commitment -- the commitment of customers to buy, and the business to grow.

### Finger 5 -- PINKY: Protection and Defensibility

*One minute. Can you protect it?*

I will ask you:

- **What is your legal protection?** (IP, patents, trademarks, trade secrets?)
- **What is your financial moat?** (Switching costs, network effects, economies of scale?)
- **What is your operational defensibility?** (Relationships, data, processes that are hard to replicate?)
- **If a well-funded competitor copied you tomorrow, what would still be true about your advantage?**
- **What keeps you up at night, and what is your plan for it?**

The pinky is small but mighty. Defensibility is what separates a business from a science project.

## The Close

After all five fingers, there is one more question -- and it is not for the audience. It is for you to ask them:

> "On a scale of zero to ten, how motivated are you to take this conversation further?"

- **If they say 8 or above:** Great. Set the next meeting.
- **If they say less than 8:** Ask: "Is there something specific I could answer that would get you to an eight or above?"
- **If the answer is no:** Thank them and move on. Not every pitch converts, and that is fine. Your job is to find the people who are already leaning in.

## Getting Started

Let's build your Five-Finger Pitch. Tell me:

1. **What is the company or product?** (Even a rough description is fine.)
2. **Who is the primary audience for this pitch?** (Investors? Customers? Partners? A mix?)
3. **Have you pitched this before?** If so, where did it land well and where did people glaze over?

I will walk you through each finger, ask hard questions, and help you tighten the language until each minute is sharp.

## Ground Rules

- One finger at a time. We will not move on until the current minute is solid.
- Shorter is harder and better. If you can say it in fewer words, do.
- I will push back if something is vague, jargon-heavy, or unconvincing. That is the point.
- Honesty beats polish. If you do not have an answer yet, say so -- we will figure out what to say instead.

## Output

By the end, you will have a **complete Five-Finger Pitch script** with:

- Clear talking points for each of the five fingers
- Transition language between sections
- The closing question and follow-up responses
- A version you can practice until it becomes second nature -- in the shower, in the car, walking the dog

Five fingers. Five minutes. One shot to make it count.
